🏛️ Politics

Senate passes revised budget reconciliation bill 51–49 after all-night session

The Senate passed a revised $1.1 trillion budget reconciliation package early Sunday morning after 22 hours of debate and a last-minute amendment on Medicaid funding that secured the final two holdout votes. The bill now moves to conference with the House, which passed its own version last Tuesday. Leadership from both chambers says they aim to resolve differences before the August recess.

The legislation includes $180 billion in new defence spending, expanded child tax credits through 2030, and contested provisions rolling back several EPA regulations. Vice President cast the tie-breaking procedural vote that kept debate moving Friday evening.

  • President signed an executive order Friday directing federal agencies to cut permitting timelines for domestic semiconductor manufacturing from 4 years to 18 months
  • NATO defence ministers met in Brussels Thursday; alliance agreed to raise baseline spending target to 2.5% of GDP
  • A federal judge in Texas blocked new DHS border enforcement rules, issuing a preliminary injunction that takes immediate effect pending appeal
📈 Markets

S&P 500 on pace for best quarter since 2023; Fed minutes signal no rush on cuts

US stocks are heading into Q2's final session near all-time highs, with the S&P 500 up 13.8% over the quarter after Friday's 0.6% gain. The rally has been driven by better-than-expected earnings, cooling core inflation (PCE came in at 2.3% last Wednesday), and easing fears of a hard landing.

Fed minutes released Thursday showed officials are "broadly comfortable" holding rates steady through summer but flagged a potential cut if labour market data softens materially. Markets are pricing in a 58% chance of a September cut, up from 34% three weeks ago.

  • S&P 500: 5,812 (+0.6% Fri) · Nasdaq: +0.9% · Dow: +0.3%
  • 10-year Treasury: 4.26%, down 8bps on the week
  • Brent crude: $81.90 — OPEC+ held output steady at Thursday's Vienna meeting
  • Bitcoin: $68,400 after pulling back from $72K mid-week

This week: ISM Manufacturing (today), JOLTS (Wednesday), and the June jobs report Friday. Consensus is 178,000 jobs added; a miss could reprice rate-cut expectations quickly.

🏆 Sports

NBA Finals tied 3–3 heading into Game 7 tonight; Wimbledon draw set

The Oklahoma City Thunder staved off elimination Friday, beating the Boston Celtics 108–99 in Game 6 to force a deciding Game 7 tonight at TD Garden (8:30 PM ET, ABC). SGA finished with 38 points and 9 assists. Jaylen Brown has been quietly dealing with a left knee bruise and was limited to 29 minutes — his availability and workload tonight will be the story heading into tip-off.

Wimbledon begins today at the All England Club. Defending champion Carlos Alcaraz and world number one Jannik Sinner are on opposite halves of the draw and are projected to meet in the final on July 13, pending results.

  • MLB weekend: Yankees swept the Red Sox in three games; Aaron Judge hit his 27th and 28th home runs Saturday
  • Copa América semis: Argentina vs. Canada (Wednesday) and Uruguay vs. Colombia (Thursday) — finals Saturday in New Jersey
  • Tour de France starts Saturday in Bilbao; Tadej Pogačar and Jonas Vingegaard lead the pre-race favourites list
🏢 Business

Apple acquires AI search startup Perplexity for $2.4B; Nike posts surprise revenue beat

Apple confirmed Friday it has acquired AI-powered search startup Perplexity for approximately $2.4 billion in an all-cash deal. The acquisition is Apple's largest since its Beats purchase in 2014 and signals an aggressive push into AI-native search for iOS and Safari. Integration is expected to begin with iOS 21 next year; the Perplexity app will remain available on Android through at least 2027.

Nike reported fiscal Q4 results Thursday that topped expectations on both revenue ($13.1B vs. $12.7B estimated) and earnings per share ($0.72 vs. $0.64). CEO Elliott Hill credited a renewed focus on performance products and a pullback from heavy discounting. Shares jumped 11% in after-hours trading — the largest single-day gain since 2020.

  • Amazon announced it is acquiring a 20% stake in satellite broadband provider AST SpaceMobile for $2.1B, expanding its logistics and connectivity ambitions
  • Volkswagen said it will close two EV battery plants in Germany and shift production to lower-cost facilities in Poland and Spain
  • The FTC filed an antitrust complaint against three major pharmacy benefit managers — CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx — alleging anti-competitive drug pricing practices
🔬 Science

Scientists confirm first room-temperature superconductor at ambient pressure; NIH approves gene therapy for sickle cell

Researchers at the University of Rochester published peer-reviewed results Thursday in Nature confirming a hydrogen-rich compound that superconducts at 22°C under standard atmospheric pressure — the long-sought "holy grail" of condensed matter physics. Independent labs in South Korea and Germany have already begun attempts to replicate the findings. If confirmed, the material could eventually transform power transmission, MRI machines, and quantum computing.

The NIH approved a new CRISPR-based gene therapy for sickle cell disease last Wednesday that works differently from the two treatments already on the market — it targets the disease's root genetic cause rather than increasing fetal haemoglobin. In trial data, 94% of participants were symptom-free at 18 months. Manufacturer expects US launch pricing around $2.1 million per patient.

  • NASA's Artemis III moon landing has been pushed to no earlier than September 2027 after a SpaceX Starship test flight anomaly last Tuesday
  • A new study in The Lancet found daily low-dose aspirin reduces colorectal cancer risk by 22% in adults over 50 — prompting calls to revisit prevention guidelines
  • NOAA reported that global average CO₂ concentration crossed 430 ppm for the first time last week, up from 421 ppm this time last year

The film was shot using real Formula 1 race weekends as a backdrop over two seasons, lending it an authenticity critics called "genuinely startling."

About us

We got tired of social media pretending to be news.

There's a specific kind of frustration that comes from spending twenty minutes on your phone first thing in the morning and walking away feeling worse informed than when you started.

That was us, every day. Open Instagram or X to "catch up," and somehow forty-five minutes later you've seen seventeen takes on yesterday's controversy, three things designed to make you angry, and approximately zero pieces of actual information you needed. Close the app. Realize you still don't know what the market did overnight or what happened in the game last night.

"We'd spend an hour between news apps and still not really know what happened yesterday."

The problem with going directly to news sites wasn't much better. Every outlet has its own view of what's "important today." Front pages are stacked with opinion columns dressed as news, five different versions of the same story, and enough breaking alerts to make anything feel urgent. Finding the two things you actually cared about meant clicking through twelve things you didn't.

We also noticed that the topics we wanted to stay on top of — markets, science, sports, politics — never lived in the same place. Multiple apps, multiple notification streams, multiple rabbit holes. Fragmented by design, because fragmentation is good for engagement numbers, even when it's bad for actual readers.

The Rooster's Crow started as a shared Google Doc. A morning summary: here's what happened in the categories we care about, written plainly, no agenda. It took us about twenty minutes each morning. After a few weeks, friends asked to be added. Then their friends. So we turned it into a newsletter.

We kept it simple on purpose. One email. Your categories. Seven o'clock. No push notifications, no algorithm, no system nudging you toward content that keeps you "engaged" for thirty more seconds. Just the things you said you wanted to read, written by people who actually read the news, arriving before your coffee gets cold.

The free tier exists because we think access to a decent morning summary of things you care about shouldn't cost anything. The paid plans exist so we can keep doing this without selling your attention to advertisers.

— Cooper & Taylor, The Rooster's Crow
