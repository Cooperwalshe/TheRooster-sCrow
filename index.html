🏛️ Politics

House passes infrastructure bill 218–210; presidential signature expected by Friday

The House passed the $940 billion infrastructure and clean energy bill Monday evening in a narrower-than-expected 218–210 vote, with four Republicans crossing the aisle to join Democrats. The bill now heads to the President's desk after the Senate's 61–38 passage last week. A signing ceremony is expected Thursday or Friday.

The final version includes $110 billion for roads and bridges, $65 billion for broadband expansion, and $47 billion for climate resilience — a number that drew the most Republican opposition in the final floor debate.

  • UK Prime Minister Starmer announced early general elections for September 18, citing a need for a "fresh mandate" on economic policy
  • The Supreme Court issued a 6–3 ruling limiting federal agency rulemaking authority in environmental cases; decision takes effect immediately
  • France and Germany reached a joint position on AI regulation ahead of next week's G7 summit in Tokyo
📈 Markets

Stocks close Q2 at record highs; S&P up 14.2% for the quarter

US equities wrapped up their best quarter in three years Monday, with the S&P 500 gaining 14.2% over the April–June period driven by stronger-than-expected earnings, cooling inflation, and renewed optimism around AI-related capital spending. The index closed at 5,847, a new all-time high.

The Fed's next meeting is July 29–30. Markets are pricing in a 62% chance of a rate cut, up from 41% a month ago. Thursday's non-farm payrolls report will be closely watched — consensus is 195,000 jobs added.

  • S&P 500: 5,847 (+0.4%) · Nasdaq: +0.6% · Dow: +0.2%
  • 10-year Treasury yield: 4.21% (down 4bps)
  • Brent crude: $83.40, up 1.1% on OPEC+ production cut signals
  • USD/EUR: 1.081 · Bitcoin: $71,200, up 3.4%

Worth watching: Q2 earnings season begins next week. JPMorgan and Wells Fargo report Friday July 11 — their guidance on consumer credit will set the tone for the sector.

🏆 Sports

Celtics win NBA Finals in 6; Argentina beats Uruguay in Copa América semis

The Boston Celtics are NBA champions for the eighteenth time, defeating the Oklahoma City Thunder 104–91 in Game 6 Monday night at TD Garden. Jaylen Brown was named Finals MVP with 26 points per game across the series. It is head coach Joe Mazzulla's first championship.

In Copa América, Argentina defeated Uruguay 2–1 in extra time in a tense semifinal in Miami. Lionel Messi scored the winner in the 104th minute on a free kick. Argentina faces Brazil in Saturday's final in Los Angeles.

  • MLB: Shohei Ohtani hit his 30th home run of the season; Dodgers lead the NL West by 7.5 games
  • Wimbledon: Quarterfinals set — Alcaraz vs. Sinner is the marquee matchup Wednesday
  • Tour de France: Stage 1 kicks off today in Lille; Tadej Pogačar is the heavy favourite
🏢 Business

Microsoft acquires enterprise AI firm Inflection Pro for $3.8B; Tesla deliveries beat Q2 estimates

Microsoft confirmed Monday it has acquired Inflection Pro, an enterprise AI assistant platform used by over 4,000 companies globally, for $3.8 billion in cash. The deal is Microsoft's largest since its Activision purchase and is expected to close by October pending regulatory review. Inflection Pro's technology will be integrated into Microsoft 365 Copilot.

Tesla reported Q2 deliveries of 468,000 vehicles, beating analyst estimates of 441,000 and reversing three straight quarters of year-on-year declines. Shares rose 8% in after-hours trading. CEO Elon Musk credited lower financing rates and the launch of the refreshed Model Y.

  • Apple's Vision Pro 2 went on sale in 14 additional countries today; pre-orders sold out within 90 minutes in most markets
  • The FTC formally approved Meta's acquisition of virtual reality startup Horizon Systems after an 18-month review
  • Airbnb raised its full-year revenue guidance by 6% after a stronger-than-expected summer booking season
🔬 Science

Oxford malaria vaccine shows 78% efficacy in Phase 3 trial; ESA launches Europa orbiter

Oxford University and the Serum Institute of India announced Monday that their R21/Matrix-M malaria vaccine demonstrated 78% efficacy across a 28,000-person Phase 3 trial in sub-Saharan Africa — the highest ever recorded for a malaria vaccine. The WHO is expected to fast-track its prequalification review, which could allow rollout to high-burden countries by mid-2027.

The European Space Agency successfully launched its JUICE (Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer) Europa module early Monday morning from the Guiana Space Centre. The spacecraft will take approximately eight years to reach Europa, where it will search for signs of liquid water beneath the moon's ice shell.

  • A Stanford study found a new class of antibiotics effective against drug-resistant tuberculosis in early-stage trials, the first meaningful TB breakthrough in two decades
  • NOAA confirmed June 2026 as the hottest June on record globally, at 1.6°C above the pre-industrial baseline
  • Researchers at MIT published a method for recycling lithium-ion batteries that recovers 96% of lithium at a fraction of current costs
🎭 Culture

Beyoncé's "Cowboy Carter" film tops Netflix; Pulitzer Prize for Fiction announced

The concert film adaptation of Beyoncé's "Cowboy Carter" album debuted on Netflix Monday and immediately became the platform's most-watched music special ever, drawing 34 million views in its first 24 hours. Shot over three nights in Houston, the film is directed by Beyoncé herself and runs two hours and eighteen minutes.

The Pulitzer Prize for Fiction was awarded Monday to Percival Everett for "James," his reimagining of Adventures of Huckleberry Finn from the perspective of Jim. Everett, who also won the Booker Prize for the novel last year, called it "a story about who gets to tell history."

About us

We got tired of social media pretending to be news.

There's a specific kind of frustration that comes from spending twenty minutes on your phone first thing in the morning and walking away feeling worse informed than when you started.

That was us, every day. Open Instagram or X to "catch up," and somehow forty-five minutes later you've seen seventeen takes on yesterday's controversy, three things designed to make you angry, and approximately zero pieces of actual information you needed. Close the app. Realize you still don't know what the market did overnight or what happened in the game last night.

"We'd spend an hour between news apps and still not really know what happened yesterday."

The problem with going directly to news sites wasn't much better. Every outlet has its own view of what's "important today." Front pages are stacked with opinion columns dressed as news, five different versions of the same story, and enough breaking alerts to make anything feel urgent. Finding the two things you actually cared about meant clicking through twelve things you didn't.

We also noticed that the topics we wanted to stay on top of — markets, science, sports, politics — never lived in the same place. Multiple apps, multiple notification streams, multiple rabbit holes. Fragmented by design, because fragmentation is good for engagement numbers, even when it's bad for actual readers.

The Rooster's Crow started as a shared Google Doc. A morning summary: here's what happened in the categories we care about, written plainly, no agenda. It took us about twenty minutes each morning. After a few weeks, friends asked to be added. Then their friends. So we turned it into a newsletter.

We kept it simple on purpose. One email. Your categories. Seven o'clock. No push notifications, no algorithm, no system nudging you toward content that keeps you "engaged" for thirty more seconds. Just the things you said you wanted to read, written by people who actually read the news, arriving before your coffee gets cold.

The free tier exists because we think access to a decent morning summary of things you care about shouldn't cost anything. The paid plans exist so we can keep doing this without selling your attention to advertisers.

— Cooper & Taylor, The Rooster's Crow
